# VOICE.md — Compass IT Solutions

> Compass speaks the way a senior tech speaks to a client they respect: direct, unhurried, never condescending. Read this file before writing any copy for the website — headlines, body text, button labels, error messages, tooltips, or meta descriptions.

---

## Core Voice Principle

**Talk like the person who fixes it.**

Compass is staffed by engineers who own outcomes, not just tickets. The writing on this site must reflect that. It means:

- Lead with the answer, not the setup.
- Use plain words for technical things.
- Use technical words only when they are load-bearing — when the precise term is genuinely the clearest option.
- Never use jargon to impress. Use it only to be accurate.

---

## Four Voice Attributes

### 01 — Direct

Lead with the answer. When there are two options, state them. When there's a recommendation, give it. Don't make the reader work to find your point.

- Bullets over paragraphs when the choice is binary or the list is genuinely parallel.
- Headlines that state the claim, not tease it.
- CTAs that say what happens when you click, not what you might get.

### 02 — Calm

Even during incidents. Confidence is communicated by tempo, not volume. The site should read at the pace of someone who has seen this before and knows exactly what to do.

- No urgency language unless there is actual urgency.
- No exclamation marks. Ever. If a claim is good enough to make, it doesn't need an exclamation mark to sell it.
- Short sentences in status updates. One fact per sentence.

### 03 — Specific

Names of things. Names of times. Names of next steps. Vague copy is a liability here — the client is trusting Compass with their infrastructure. Specific language builds that trust before the first call.

- "Your backups completed overnight." Not "Your systems are protected."
- "Plan to be without printing for one hour." Not "There may be some disruption."
- "Replacement is on-site, swap window is 14:00–14:30." Not "We'll handle it shortly."

### 04 — Quietly warm

A small human note earns its keep. A short acknowledgement, a dry observation, a brief moment of honesty. But only where it fits naturally — never forced, never cutesy.

- "That one's on us." — honest ownership of a mistake.
- "We don't disappear after deployment." — a simple human promise.
- Never: "We're so excited to work with you!!" or "Our amazing team of rockstar engineers!!"

## Language Rules

### Always write in second-person singular or first-person plural

| Use | Avoid |
|---|---|
| "We isolate the issue" | "Compass IT Solutions isolates the issue" |
| "Your infrastructure" | "The client's infrastructure" |
| "We'll scope the engagement" | "An engagement will be scoped" |

### No passive voice

| Use | Avoid |
|---|---|
| "We labelled every cable." | "Every cable was labelled." |
| "We document every config." | "All configs are documented." |

### Expand acronyms on first use, then use the short form

If the acronym is not universal (i.e. not TCP/IP, DNS, API), spell it out the first time it appears on the page. After that, the short form is acceptable.

- First use: "Managed Service Provider (MSP)" → thereafter: "MSP"
- First use: "Service Level Agreement (SLA)" → thereafter: "SLA"
- Universal and don't need expansion: TCP/IP, DNS, VPN, API, UI, UX, CMS

### No fear-selling

Compass does not lead with threat. The business earns trust by demonstrating competence, not by making the reader feel their current setup is a disaster.

| Use | Avoid |
|---|---|
| "We architect for redundancy from the ground up." | "Your network is one outage away from disaster." |
| "Quarterly reviews catch drift before it becomes an incident." | "Without proper monitoring, your business is at risk." |

### Numbers, dates, and times

- Use numerals for all numbers 10 and above: "30+ engineers", "24/7 on-call", "99.9% uptime".
- Spell out numbers one through nine when not paired with a unit: "two options", "six steps".
- Time: 24-hour format for technical contexts (`14:00–14:30`), 12-hour with AM/PM for marketing copy (`2:00–2:30 PM`).
- Dates: Day Month Year format (`14 April 2026`) in running text. ISO format (`2026-04-14`) in technical/code contexts.

## We Say / We Don't Say

| We say | We don't say |
|---|---|
| "Your backups completed overnight." | "Your data is safe and secure thanks to our robust systems." |
| "Here are the two options. Here's what we'd choose." | "We offer a wide range of flexible solutions to meet your needs." |
| "Plan to be without printing for an hour." | "There may be some temporary service disruption during the maintenance window." |
| "That one's on us." | "We apologise for any inconvenience caused." |

| "The relationship starts when the network goes live." | "Our long-term partnership approach ensures continued success." |
| "Go-live is a checkpoint, not a finish line." | "We offer comprehensive end-to-end managed IT solutions." |
| "30-day diagnostic — risk register, system map, quick wins — before we propose anything long-term." | "Our proprietary assessment methodology enables us to holistically evaluate your IT ecosystem." |

## Status Update Writing Standard

When writing status updates (for a status page, incident communication, or maintenance notice):

**Structure:** What happened · When · What we did · What happens next.

**Example — correct:**
> We isolated the issue to the upstream switch at 09:42. Replacement is on-site, swap window is 14:00–14:30. Email will go out when service is back.

**Example — wrong:**
> We are currently experiencing an issue with our network infrastructure. Our dedicated team is working around the clock to investigate and resolve this as quickly as possible. We apologise for any inconvenience this may cause and appreciate your patience.

## Headlines — Pattern Guide

| Section type | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Hero / banner | Bold claim · specific, not generic | "Wired right. Kept running." |
| Service page title | Service name + plain-spoken subhead | "IT Services / The IT team behind your IT team." |
| About / brand statement | Present tense, first person | "We build the quiet infrastructure that lets ambitious teams move without friction." |
| Pillar / feature | Short noun phrase | "Wired right." / "Plain-spoken." / "In it for the run." |
| Social proof / stat | Number + terse label | "30+ engineers" / "99.9% SLA" |
| CTA headline | Direct instruction or direct promise | "Get in touch." / "Start with a 30-day diagnostic." |

## Button & CTA Copy Rules

- **CTAs state the next concrete step**, not a vague aspiration.
- **No "Learn more"** as a standalone CTA — add specificity: "See how we work" or "Read the network services overview".
- **No "Submit"** on contact forms — use "Send your message" or "Start the conversation".
- **No "Click here"** — ever.

| Use | Avoid |
|---|---|
| "Start with a diagnostic" | "Get started" |
| "See the network infrastructure service" | "Learn more" |
| "Send your message" | "Submit" |
| "Call +974 5149 0825" | "Contact us" (alone, without detail) |

## Error Messages & Microcopy

Apply the same voice to error states, empty states, form validation, and tooltips.

| Situation | Copy |
|---|---|
| Form field empty | "Add your name before we send this." |
| Invalid email | "Check the email address — something looks off." |
| Form submission success | "Message sent. We'll be in touch within one business day." |
| 404 page | "This page doesn't exist. If you followed a link here, let us know: asahli@compass-its.com" |
| Loading state | "One moment." |

## What Never Appears on This Site

- Exclamation marks (anywhere, ever)
- "World-class", "cutting-edge", "state-of-the-art", "industry-leading", "best-in-class"
- "Synergy", "leverage", "ecosystem", "holistic", "robust solutions", "seamless experience"
- "Rockstar", "ninja", "guru", "wizard" (as role descriptors)
- "Per my last email"
- Rhetorical questions used as headlines ("Are you tired of IT headaches?")
- "Don't worry, we've got this!!"
- Emoji in formal copy (acceptable in direct/informal social only)
